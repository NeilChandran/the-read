CS109 Write-Up — Methodology & Proofs

1. Modeling Population Skill with the Beta Distribution

Self-ratings on a 1–10 scale are normalized to the unit interval via x = (r − 1) / 9, placing "exactly average" (r = 5) at x = 0.444. We model the population distribution of normalized self-ratings as X ~ Beta(α, β).

f(x; α, β) = x^(α−1) · (1−x)^(β−1) / B(α, β) where B(α, β) = Γ(α)Γ(β) / Γ(α+β) is the Beta function.

Given n aggregate observations {x₁, …, xₙ} (from localStorage), we estimate α and β via method-of-moments MLE. Let μ̂ = (1/n)Σxᵢ and σ̂² = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ − μ̂)². Matching moments:

E[X] = α/(α+β) = μ̂ Var[X] = αβ / [(α+β)²(α+β+1)] = σ̂² Solving: t = μ̂(1 − μ̂)/σ̂² − 1 α̂ = μ̂ · t β̂ = (1 − μ̂) · t

When σ̂² is too small (degenerate data), we fall back to α = β = 2 (uniform-ish prior).

2. Bayesian Update: Prior + Likelihood → Posterior

For each trait, the prior on true ability θ is θ ~ Beta(α, β) fitted from aggregate self-ratings. The user's self-rating r implies a noisy observation of true ability. Historical calibration analysis shows self-assessors systematically overestimate: the population mean normalized rating μ̂ typically exceeds 0.444 (the "average" anchor).

We estimate a calibration parameter δ̂ = max(0, μ̂ − 0.444) via MLE from aggregate data — the average overconfidence shift. The adjusted observation is:

x_adj = clamp(x_user − δ̂, ε, 1−ε) where x_user = (r − 1)/9 and ε = 0.001.

Treating x_adj as pseudo-evidence with weight w = 2 (conjugate Beta update):

Posterior: θ | data ~ Beta(α + w·x_adj, β + w·(1 − x_adj))

The posterior probability of being genuinely above average (top 50%) is:

P(θ > 0.5 | data) = 1 − I_{0.5}(α′, β′) where I_x(a,b) is the regularized incomplete Beta function (CDF of Beta(a,b)).

3. Why the Lake Wobegon Effect Is Mathematically Impossible

The Lake Wobegon effect claims "everyone is above average." Formally, let N people each claim skill sᵢ relative to the population mean μ. If everyone is above average, then sᵢ > μ for all i.

μ = (1/N) Σ sᵢ If sᵢ > μ for all i, then: μ = (1/N) Σ sᵢ > (1/N) Σ μ = μ Contradiction: μ > μ. ∎

More generally, if fraction p > 50% claim to be above median, the median of self-reports shifts upward — but the true median of ability is fixed. At most 50% of any distribution can lie strictly above its median. Our aggregate data shows 93.2% of visitors rated above average on ≥ 7 of 10 questions, which violates this constraint.

4. Geometric Mean Aggregation

We combine per-trait posterior probabilities P₁, …, P₁₀ using the geometric mean:

G = (P₁ · P₂ · … · P₁₀)^(1/10)

The geometric mean is appropriate here because: (a) probabilities are multiplicative under independent evidence across traits; (b) it penalizes a single very low posterior more harshly than the arithmetic mean — one domain of clear overconfidence drags the overall score down; (c) it stays in [0, 1] without normalization. The arithmetic mean would let one high trait compensate excessively for overconfidence elsewhere.
